ABSTRACT 

The digital landscape is increasingly reliant on robust security mechanisms, with cryptography serving as a 

foundational pillar for data confidentiality, integrity, and authentication. However, traditional cryptographic systems 

face escalating challenges from advanced cyber threats and the advent of quantum computing. This paper explores the 

transformative integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) with cryptography, examining its dual role in both enhancing 

cryptanalytic capabilities and revolutionizing cryptographic design and protocol enhancement. The discussion 

encompasses AI's application in distinguishing attacks, side-channel analysis, and key recovery against simplified 

ciphers, while also detailing its inherent limitations against modern, high-entropy cryptographic algorithms. 

Concurrently, the paper investigates AI's contributions to cryptographic primitive design, adaptive key management, 

privacy-preserving computation, and the formal verification of AI-generated components. Challenges such as 

computational overhead, data dependency, interpretability, and ethical considerations are critically analysed. A 

conceptual framework for a hybrid AI-cryptographic system is proposed, emphasizing adaptive security and a 

seamless transition to post-quantum cryptography. This comprehensive review highlights AI's potential to forge more 

resilient, adaptive, and privacy-centric cryptographic solutions for the future.

1. INTRODUCTION 

A. Background and Significance of Cryptography in the Digital Era 

The contemporary digital era is characterized by an unprecedented reliance on digital communication and data storage, 

elevating information security to a paramount concern for individuals, businesses, and governments worldwide. At the 

core of this security infrastructure lies cryptography, a discipline fundamental for safeguarding sensitive information. 

Cryptography forms the bedrock of essential security mechanisms, including data encryption and identity 

authentication. Its enduring principles underpin secure communication, ensuring confidentiality, preserving data 

integrity, verifying authentication, establishing non-repudiation, and facilitating robust key management [1]. 

Confidentiality, a cornerstone of secure communication, is primarily achieved through encryption techniques, 

restricting access to information only to authorized parties and maintaining privacy. Authentication mechanisms, such 

as access tokens and authentication signatures, are crucial for confirming the genuine identity of a message sender, 

thereby ensuring that data originates from a trusted source. Data integrity guarantees that information remains 

consistent and accurate throughout its lifecycle, preventing any unauthorized alterations during transit or storage, often 

enforced through cryptographic hashing. Non-repudiation provides undeniable proof of origin, preventing a sender 

from falsely denying the transmission of a message or the signing of a document, a function typically fulfilled by 

digital signatures. Finally, effective key management, encompassing tasks like key distribution, generation, and 

rotation, is indispensable for the overall security of any cryptographic system, as its strength is intrinsically linked to 

the proper handling of cryptographic keys [2]. 

The increasing reliance on digital platforms elevates cryptography from a purely technical discipline to a foundational 

pillar of modern society and economy. The exponential growth in digital communication and storage underscores that 

cryptography's impact extends far beyond mere data protection; it is integral to the very functioning of contemporary 

society. Consequently, any compromise in cryptographic security carries systemic implications, rather than isolated 

consequences. However, despite their foundational role and proven effectiveness, traditional cryptographic systems 

face escalating pressure from an evolving threat landscape. Emerging threats, including the rapid advancement of 

quantum computing, increasingly sophisticated cyberattacks, and persistent vulnerabilities in key management 

processes, necessitate continuous innovation and adaptation in cryptographic methods. This inherent vulnerability in

immutable bedrock of secure digital communication, and any integration of AI must fundamentally uphold or enhance 

them, rather than introduce new vulnerabilities. 

Confidentiality: This principle ensures that information remains private and accessible only to authorized individuals. 

It is typically achieved through encryption techniques, which transform data into an unreadable format, thereby

2. Overview of Cryptographic Primitives: Symmetric-Key, Asymmetric-Key, Hash Functions, and Digital 

Signatures 

Cryptographic primitives are the fundamental building blocks used to construct more complex cryptographic 

protocols. Their inherent mathematical properties and computational hardness assumptions are precisely the targets AI 

aims to either exploit (in cryptanalysis) or optimize and leverage (in cryptographic design). 

Symmetric Cryptography: This type of cryptography utilizes a single, shared secret key for both encrypting plaintext 

into ciphertext and decrypting ciphertext back into plaintext. Examples of widely used symmetric-key encryption 

algorithms include the Advanced Encryption Standard (AES), Data Encryption Standard (DES), Triple Data 

Encryption Standard (Triple DES), and the International Data Encryption Algorithm (IDEA). A significant practical 

challenge with symmetric-key encryption is the secure distribution and sharing of this secret key among all 

communicating parties, which can introduce substantial overhead and complexity for cryptographic applications. 

Asymmetric Cryptography (Public-Key Cryptography): In contrast to symmetric cryptography, asymmetric 

cryptography employs distinct cryptographic keys for encryption and decryption: a public key, which is widely shared, 

and a private key, which is kept secret. Although there is a mathematical connection between these key pairs, the 

private key cannot be derived from the public key. Examples include Elliptical Curve Cryptography (ECC), Rivest 

Shamir Adleman (RSA), and the Diffie-Hellman exchange method. Public-key cryptography was specifically invented 

to address the complex key management problems associated with symmetric-key systems, particularly the secure key 

exchange. It is commonly employed in digital signatures for message authentication, where senders use their private 

keys to digitally sign messages, allowing receivers to verify authenticity with the corresponding public key. 

Hash Functions: Cryptographic hash functions are one-way transformations that take an input message of arbitrary 

length and produce a fixed-size output, known as a hash value or message digest. These functions are designed to be 

computationally infeasible to reverse (the one-way property) and to prevent finding two distinct inputs that produce 

the same hash output (the collision-free property). A crucial characteristic is the "avalanche effect," where even a 

slight alteration in the input string should cause a dramatic and unpredictable change in the hash output. 

Digital Signatures: Built upon asymmetric cryptography, digital signatures provide robust mechanisms for 

authentication and non-repudiation. They allow a sender to digitally sign a message with their private key, creating a 

unique signature that can then be verified by anyone possessing the sender's public key, thereby guaranteeing the 

message's origin and integrity. 

The core idea is that these primitives are not arbitrary; they are meticulously designed based on specific mathematical 

problems believed to be intractable (e.g., factoring for RSA, discrete logarithm for Diffie-Hellman). AI's interaction 

with these primitives is therefore a direct engagement with these mathematical structures—either to find weaknesses 

in their implementation or to design new structures with desired properties. A significant conceptual tension arises 

from the design of strong, modern ciphers, which aim to produce outputs indistinguishable from true randomness 

(high entropy). This fundamentally challenges AI's core strength of pattern recognition. Encrypted data must appear 

random, a characteristic that can cause machine learning algorithms to fail, as neural networks trained with random 

data find no correlation. This inherent contradiction establishes a fundamental limitation for AI in direct cryptanalysis 

of ideal ciphers [7].

1. Machine Learning and Deep Learning for Cryptanalysis 

The fields of machine learning (ML) and cryptanalysis share a common objective: creating a function based on a given 

set of inputs and outputs. In cryptanalysis, this often translates to finding the decryption function or key from 

plaintext-ciphertext pairs. Deep learning models, particularly neural networks, excel at pattern recognition and 

complex tasks, making them inherently suitable for such analytical challenges. The fundamental conceptual alignment 

between machine learning's goal of "learning an unknown function" and cryptanalysis's goal of "inverting a 

cryptographic function" positions AI as a potent, albeit complex, tool for codebreaking. The "black box" nature of 

strong ciphers, which aim to obscure internal operations, is ironically met by the "black box" learning capabilities of 

deep learning, where the AI attempts to learn the underlying mapping without explicit programming [8-10]. 

A key advantage of deep learning algorithms in this context is their ability to directly process raw data, thereby 

eliminating the need for predefined feature selection or extraction, a common requirement in traditional ML 

approaches. This capacity for automated feature extraction, bypassing the laborious and often subjective process of 

manual feature engineering, significantly enhances AI's potential to uncover subtle and complex cryptographic 

weaknesses. This implies that AI can potentially discover patterns or biases that are too intricate or subtle for human 

cryptanalysts to identify manually, thereby extending the reach of cryptanalytic attacks. This capability allows neural 

networks to assist cryptanalysts in identifying vulnerabilities by training these networks with data that highlights 

inherent weaknesses within cryptographic schemes. 

a. Distinguishing Attacks and Pattern Recognition in Ciphertexts 

AI and Machine Learning (ML) have significantly accelerated pattern recognition capabilities, which can be leveraged 

to aid attackers in cryptanalysis. Neural networks are adept at estimating distributions and capturing complex 

dependencies between variables, making them suitable for analysing cryptographic outputs. A novel application 

involves using ML models to classify ciphers, effectively acting as an adversary in an Indistinguishability Under 

Chosen Plaintext Attack (IND-CPA) setting, by employing entropy-based distribution estimation. This classification 

model has successfully identified encryption schemes that are not IND-CPA secure, such as DES, RSA, and AES in 

ECB mode, with high accuracy. This demonstrates AI's ability to discern patterns that betray a cipher's deterministic 

behaviour. Furthermore, it can pinpoint vulnerabilities in otherwise CPA-secure cryptosystems when faulty parameters 

are present, such as a reduced counter version of AES-CTR or OAEP RSA with reused padding. 

Mutual Information Neural Estimation (MINE) is employed to calculate a cryptosystem's mutual information leakage, 

where a high MI value indicates significant information leakage between plaintext and ciphertext. This provides an 

empirical lower bound on security, which is often more practical and attainable than formal theoretical guarantees for 

real-world systems. A distinguisher, generally, is a technique used to discern encrypted text from random noise, 

serving as the crucial first step in launching a key recovery attack. Notably, deep learning-based pseudorandom 

distinguishers developed for lightweight ciphers like Speck and Simon have achieved very high average accuracies 

(99.5% for Speck and 99.6% for Simon) in distinguishing their outputs from truly random sequences. The ability of AI 

to detect subtle patterns and information leakage, even in outputs designed to appear random, fundamentally 

challenges the assumption of perfect pseudo randomness in cryptographic designs, pushing the boundaries of what 

constitutes a "secure" cipher. Since cryptographic primitives are designed to produce outputs that are computationally 

indistinguishable from true randomness, AI's capability to detect deviations from this ideal is a direct and potent attack 

vector. This implies that even if a cipher appears statistically sound to human analysis or traditional tests, AI might 

uncover subtle, exploitable patterns, necessitating a re-evaluation of pseudo randomness criteria in cryptographic 

design [11]. 

b. Side-Channel Attacks: Exploiting Physical Leakages with AI 

Deep learning can be effectively applied in side-channel attacks (SCAs), which exploit inadvertent information 

leakage during the encryption process, such as power consumption or electromagnetic radiation. AI significantly 

amplifies the threat of side-channel attacks by automating the complex process of feature extraction and enhancing 

noise resilience, thereby making SCAs more accessible, efficient, and potent against real-world implementations. 

Traditional SCAs often require expert-crafted features, but AI can automatically detect subtle patterns in side-channel 

leakage, eliminating the need for manual feature engineering and drastically lowering the barrier to entry for attackers. 

AI models can detect unusual patterns in cryptographic executions that may indicate an ongoing attack. Compared to 

traditional methods, deep learning has demonstrated superior efficiency and accuracy in SCAs. Notably, Inception 

Net-based network structures for SCAs utilize fewer training parameters and achieve faster convergence through 

parallel processing of input data. To counteract noise, LU-Net-based networks have been introduced for denoising

c. Key Recovery Techniques: Successes and Limitations against Simplified Ciphers 

Deep learning models have been proposed to predict the secret key of a cipher by taking plaintexts and their 

corresponding ciphertexts as input. For Simplified AES (S-AES), a multi-label classification (MLC) approach for 

cryptanalysis has shown to significantly outperform a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) approach. The MLC model 

achieved a 68% total key retrieval rate on S-AES, with certain key bits proving particularly vulnerable. Deep learning-

based key recovery has been demonstrated for S-AES and S-SPECK, capable of recovering keys up to 12-bit and 6-bit 

lengths, respectively. 

While AI demonstrates promising key recovery capabilities against simplified or round-reduced ciphers, its 

effectiveness diminishes drastically against full-round, modern ciphers. This is due to their design principles, which 

prioritize high entropy, computational complexity, and the absence of exploitable patterns. A crucial finding is that for 

modern cryptographic algorithms with full rounds and full keys, deep learning-based key recovery is currently 

considered "practically infeasible". This infeasibility is attributed to the fact that strong encryption relies on complex 

mathematical operations that are computationally infeasible to reverse without the proper key. Additional challenges 

for deep learning in cracking encryption include the lack of large, labelled datasets (due to the confidential nature of 

cryptographic information) and generalization issues across diverse and adaptive encryption schemes. 

A novel vulnerability arises from the fundamental discrepancy between the discrete nature of cryptographic operations 

and the continuous computational model of Deep Neural Networks. This allows non-standard inputs to compromise 

DNN implementations of ciphers, irrespective of the underlying cipher's mathematical strength. Deep neural networks, 

which are designed to process continuous real numbers, can be broken in linear time by non-standard (non-binary) 

inputs when implementing block ciphers, even full-round AES-128. This means that while a cryptographic algorithm 

(e.g., AES) is designed for binary inputs, its implementation within a DNN, which operates on real numbers, can be 

exploited by an attacker providing non-binary inputs. This represents a new attack surface created by the integration of 

AI, highlighting that even strong ciphers can become vulnerable through improper AI integration [13]. 

2. Neural Cryptography: Concepts and Applications in Cryptanalysis 

Neural cryptography is a specialized branch of cryptography dedicated to analysing the application of stochastic 

algorithms, particularly artificial neural networks (ANNs), for use in both encryption and cryptanalysis. ANNs are 

recognized for their ability to selectively explore solution spaces, making them potentially suitable for cryptanalysis 

by finding the inverse function of cryptographic algorithms. The concepts of mutual learning, self-learning, and the 

stochastic behaviour of neural networks can be applied to various cryptographic aspects, including public-key 

cryptography, key distribution (via neural network mutual synchronization), hashing, or pseudo-random number 

generation [14]. 

A key application within this field is the Neural Key Exchange protocol, which aims to serve as a secure replacement 

for the widely used Diffie-Hellman protocol by synchronizing two Tree Parity Machines (TPMs). In this protocol, 

legitimate communicating parties are designed to synchronize their TPMs faster than an eavesdropping attacker can 

learn the shared key. This security advantage is enhanced by increasing the synaptic depth of the neural network, 

making it computationally infeasible for an attacker to derive the key within a practical timeframe. Furthermore, a 

significant characteristic of neural key exchange is that it is not based on number theory, which suggests potential 

resilience against future quantum computing attacks that threaten traditional number-theoretic cryptographic schemes.

3. Inherent Limitations and Challenges of AI in Cryptanalysis against Modern Ciphers 

Despite AI's advancements in cryptanalysis, significant inherent limitations and challenges persist when these 

techniques are applied against robust, modern cryptographic algorithms. 

a. Resistance due to High Entropy and Lack of Exploitable Patterns 

Modern cryptographic algorithms are meticulously designed to achieve a high degree of security, primarily through 

maximizing entropy and eliminating exploitable patterns in their outputs. Strong encryption relies on complex 

mathematical operations that are computationally infeasible to reverse without the proper key. Encrypted data is 

designed to appear random, a characteristic that inherently causes machine learning algorithms to struggle. Neural 

networks trained with truly random data find no correlation, which is a fundamental barrier to their effectiveness. 

Entropy, a measure of randomness or unpredictability, is crucial in cryptography for generating secure keys; without a 

good source of entropy, cryptographic protocols become vulnerable to attacks exploiting predictability. 

The fundamental design goal of modern cryptographic algorithms is to maximize entropy and eliminate exploitable 

patterns, creating an environment that inherently negates AI's core strength of pattern recognition, thus posing a 

significant barrier to direct cryptanalysis. The more random and pattern-free a ciphertext is, the less information AI 

can extract. The concept of "perfect secrecy," where the ciphertext reveals no information about the plaintext, or 

"semantic security," where any revealed information cannot be feasibly extracted, represents the theoretical limit of 

this design philosophy. Claude Shannon proved that perfect secrecy is unbreakable if the key length is at least as long 

as the message and truly random. This implies that AI is not breaking the mathematics of strong ciphers but rather 

exploiting implementation flaws or simplified versions. 

While practical perfect secrecy is challenging to achieve, computationally secure cryptosystems relax assumptions to 

achieve practical security, aiming for zero mutual information between plaintext and ciphertext. However, it is 

extremely challenging to calculate information-theoretic metrics like mutual information for complex cryptographic 

protocols. The computational difficulty of accurately measuring information leakage in complex, high-entropy 

cryptographic protocols further limits AI's ability to identify subtle weaknesses, pushing cryptanalysis towards 

empirical bounds rather than theoretical breaks. This suggests that AI's role in cryptanalysis of strong ciphers might be 

more about identifying distinguishers or empirical vulnerabilities rather than achieving a "total break" or a 

fundamental undermining of the cryptographic primitive's mathematical security. 

b. Barriers Posed by Computational Hardness Assumptions 

Cryptographic algorithms are fundamentally designed around computational hardness assumptions, which hypothesize 

that certain mathematical problems cannot be solved efficiently (in polynomial time). These problems include integer 

factorization (e.g., for RSA), the discrete logarithm problem (e.g., for Diffie-Hellman), and the Learning with Errors 

(LWE) problem (a basis for post-quantum cryptography). The security of many cryptographic protocols relies on these 

assumptions, meaning that for AI to "break" a provably secure cipher in a fundamental way (i.e., not via 

implementation flaws), it would need to solve these mathematically hard problems. Since these problems are 

conjectured to be intractable even for classical computers, AI's pattern recognition, while powerful, does not inherently 

provide a shortcut to solving problems that are mathematically defined as "hard" in polynomial time. 

Provable security, a well-established paradigm in cryptography, relies on these computational assumptions to 

demonstrate that breaking a scheme is at least as difficult as solving an underlying hard problem through a "proof by 

reduction". This implies that if an efficient adversary could break the cryptographic scheme, it could also efficiently 

solve the assumed hard problem, leading to a contradiction. This mathematical framework represents a fundamental, 

theoretical barrier to AI-based cryptanalysis, as AI, like any other computational approach, is bound by the theoretical 

intractability of these underlying problems. 

The advent of quantum computing necessitates a shift to post-quantum cryptography (PQC), based on different 

hardness assumptions. While quantum computers can efficiently solve factoring and discrete logarithm problems, 

lattice problems (like LWE) are conjectured to remain hard even for quantum computers, making them prime 

candidates for PQC. This transition introduces new challenges for AI, forcing AI-based cryptanalysis to adapt to 

different mathematical structures and potentially new forms of intractability. This creates a continuous "arms race" in

c. Data Dependency, Generalization, and Interpretability Issues of AI Models 

The practical application of AI models in cryptanalysis against real-world, diverse, and protected cryptographic 

implementations faces significant hurdles related to data. Deep learning models require large-scale, high-quality 

training datasets to be effective in cryptanalysis. Collecting sufficient data for this purpose is extremely demanding 

and resource intensive. For instance, robust training of side-channel attack models might necessitate hundreds of 

thousands to millions of power traces, with some researchers noting that even extensive datasets (e.g., 65,000 

compressed power traces) might be inadequate against advanced countermeasures, potentially requiring datasets 

exceeding 1TB. Many deep learning models assume attackers have unrestricted access to cryptographic devices for 

extensive sample gathering, which is often unrealistic in passive or limited-access attack environments. Existing 

datasets frequently lack diversity, leading to model overfitting and severely impairing their effectiveness when 

encountering variations in device-specific noise patterns or cryptographic implementations. The inherent data-hungry 

nature and generalization challenges of AI models thus pose significant practical barriers to their effective application 

in cryptanalysis against real-world, diverse, and protected cryptographic implementations. 

Furthermore, AI models often function as "black boxes," limiting interpretability in cryptanalysis. When a neural 

network identifies correct key guesses, it is often unclear which cryptographic weaknesses the model is exploiting. 

This lack of interpretability hinders cryptographers' ability to identify underlying vulnerabilities and improve 

algorithms, and it increases the risk of false positives, as AI models might rely on coincidental statistical correlations. 

The "black box" problem of AI models introduces a critical interpretability gap in cryptanalysis, hindering the 

fundamental goal of understanding cryptographic weaknesses and simultaneously creating new security vulnerabilities 

through adversarial manipulation of the AI systems themselves. AI systems themselves can become security 

vulnerabilities if subjected to adversarial attacks or data poisoning during training phases, potentially producing 

intentionally misleading outcomes. This means the very tool designed to find weaknesses can become a source of new

C. AI in Cryptographic Design and Enhancement 

Beyond its role in cryptanalysis, AI is increasingly being leveraged to enhance cryptographic design, optimize existing 

algorithms, and develop novel security protocols. This represents a proactive application of AI to strengthen digital 

defences. 

1. AI in Cryptographic Primitive Design 

AI techniques are being explored for the automated design and optimization of cryptographic primitives, which are the 

fundamental building blocks of secure systems. 

a. S-box and Boolean Function Design 

Substitution boxes (S-boxes) are crucial non-linear components in symmetric-key block ciphers, responsible for 

confusion and resistance against various attacks, including side-channel attacks. Designing cryptographically secure 

and energy-efficient S-boxes is a complex task. Supervised machine learning approaches are being used to automate 

the determination and verification of the dynamic power efficiency of S-boxes, showing a 4% increase in accuracy for 

power prediction. This automation addresses the inaccuracy and sluggishness of traditional methods, enabling faster 

and more accurate evaluation of S-box performance. Furthermore, machine learning can be used to construct S-boxes 

that are both cryptographically secure and low-power, by estimating their dynamic strength through optimization 

strategies. 

Deep neural networks are also being applied to predict the number of active S-boxes in block ciphers, a critical 

measure for resistance against differential cryptanalysis. This data-driven approach trades off exactness for real-time 

efficiency by shifting computational work to the training phase. Boolean functions, essential cryptographic primitives 

in symmetric cryptography, also benefit from AI in their design. Evolutionary algorithms, for instance, are used to 

search for cryptographically sound Boolean functions with desired properties like high nonlinearity, which is vital for 

resisting linear cryptanalysis. These methods have shown to yield better results than random search for finding 

nonlinear Boolean functions. 

b. Hash Function Generation 

The effective generation of hash functions is paramount for network security. Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) have 

been proposed as a viable approach for cryptographic hash function generation. A multi-layer feed-forward network 

can be configured for this purpose, with an input layer corresponding to message blocks, hidden layers for processing, 

and an output layer for the fixed-size hash value. The number of neurons in the hidden layer can be optimized to 

enhance performance. 

A main advantage of using ANNs for hash function generation is security: if the same secret parameters of the ANN 

(synapses and bias values) are used, it becomes very difficult to reconstruct them from transferred messages, implying 

strong resistance against attacks. The proposed solutions can also be realized in a parallel manner, contributing to 

faster hash function generation. Furthermore, the behaviour of the ANN can be dynamically changed by randomly

c. Evolutionary Algorithms for Primitive Design 

Evolutionary computation, inspired by biological mechanisms of evolution, offers a nature-inspired approach to 

address complex problems, including cryptographic primitive design. Genetic algorithms (GAs), genetic programming

2. AI in Cryptographic Protocol Enhancement 

AI is not only being used to design primitives but also to enhance existing cryptographic protocols and mechanisms, 

making them more adaptive and resilient. 

a. AI-Enhanced Key Exchange Protocols 

Key exchange protocols are fundamental for establishing secure communication channels. AI-driven approaches are 

being investigated to improve post-quantum cryptographic (PQC) key exchange protocols, making them more 

efficient, secure, and scalable. Machine learning models can assist in optimizing or predicting optimal parameter 

choices for better key exchange in practice, particularly for lattice-based cryptography which is resistant to quantum 

threats. A hybrid AI model can minimize computational overhead in key exchange protocols and dynamically adjust 

cryptographic parameters based on current side-channel attacks, making the system resilient and reducing 

communication latency through deep learning algorithms. This has shown significant improvements, with AI-driven 

models improving key exchange speed by 27% over traditional PQC techniques while maintaining robust security. 

Beyond PQC, AI and Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) are being leveraged to enhance the complexity and 

resistance of traditional hybrid encryption algorithms, such as combinations of Polybius and Vigenère Ciphers. ANNs 

play a critical role in dynamically generating and optimizing cipher parameters, designing custom Polybius grids, and 

analysing character frequency to derive keys or decryption methods, thereby enhancing the system's resilience against 

vulnerabilities. 

b. Adaptive Encryption and Parameter Optimization 

Adaptive encryption frameworks, particularly in resource-constrained environments like wireless sensor networks 

(WSNs), are leveraging reinforcement learning (RL) to dynamically scale encryption levels based on real-time 

network conditions and threat classification. This approach integrates deep learning-based anomaly detection to 

classify network states (low, moderate, high threat) and guides encryption policy selection. Dynamic Q-learning 

optimizes energy efficiency in low-threat conditions, while double Q-learning provides robust security adaptation in 

high-threat environments. A Hybrid Policy Derivation Algorithm balances encryption complexity and computational 

overhead by dynamically switching between these learning models. Such RL-optimized implementations have 

demonstrated significant improvements, including up to 30% faster execution times and 20% lower memory 

consumption compared to standard baselines for post-quantum algorithms. This framework enhances the practicality 

of PQC schemes by reducing resource demands, accelerating their adoption in real-world applications.

attack and help design secure implementations by analysing vulnerabilities and suggesting improvements like adding 

randomness or masking techniques. For instance, AI models can introduce randomized noise into timing, power 

consumption, or electromagnetic signals to disrupt attack patterns, or adaptively randomize execution order and

3. AI for Privacy-Preserving Cryptography 

As data privacy becomes increasingly critical, AI is being integrated with cryptographic techniques to enable 

computations on sensitive data without compromising its confidentiality. 

a. Secure Multi-Party Computation (SMPC) 

Secure Multi-Party Computation (SMPC) is a cryptographic technique that enables multiple parties to jointly compute 

a function over their private inputs without revealing those inputs to each other. AI can significantly enhance 

blockchain systems by enabling smarter decision-making and real-time anomaly detection, improving fraud 

prevention mechanisms, and streamlining security protocols within SMPC frameworks. AI systems can continuously 

monitor blockchain transactions, identify suspicious behaviour patterns, and autonomously respond to potential threats 

in real-time. AI's predictive capabilities can anticipate future security breaches, enabling proactive risk mitigation. 

The integration of AI into blockchain technology, particularly for SMPC, opens new opportunities to strengthen 

security and automate key processes. AI leverages machine learning algorithms to analyse large volumes of data in 

real-time, identifying anomalies or patterns that could indicate potential security threats, such as fraud or unauthorized 

access. This proactive monitoring helps prevent fraud and ensures data security. AI-powered smart contracts offer a 

sophisticated means of automating security enforcement and decision-making on the blockchain. SMPC can enable 

privacy-preserving machine learning, where models can be trained on decentralized datasets without compromising 

privacy, exemplified by the integration with Federated Learning. However, challenges include scalability, as 

cryptographic operations can be time-consuming and computationally expensive, especially for large datasets or many 

participants. 

b. Homomorphic Encryption (HE) 

Homomorphic Encryption (HE) is a powerful cryptographic technique that allows computations to be performed 

directly on encrypted data, yielding an encrypted result that, when decrypted, is the same as if the operations were 

performed on plaintext. This is a significant departure from traditional encryption, which only protects data at rest or 

in transit, leaving it vulnerable during processing when it must be decrypted. HE is categorized into Partially 

Homomorphic Encryption (PHE) supporting one type of operation, Somewhat Homomorphic Encryption (SHE) 

supporting a finite number of operations, and Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) allowing arbitrary computations 

of unbounded depth. 

HE offers transformative potential for AI and Machine Learning (ML) models, particularly in sensitive domains like 

healthcare, by enabling secure collaboration and data sharing without compromising privacy. It facilitates the 

leveraging of multi-institutional datasets for AI tools, improving the accuracy and utility of predictions while adhering 

to stringent data privacy regulations. This protects confidential information used to train AI/ML models and for 

generative AI queries and outputs from malicious access and exploitation. If threat actors bypass other security 

measures, FHE ensures that accessed data is unusable and cannot be tampered with or poisoned. 

Despite its advantages, HE presents significant computational challenges. It is computationally intensive, leading to 

slower performance and extended processing times, which can delay AI/ML outcomes and introduce governance 

challenges for timely decision-making. Implementing HE also requires specialized expertise and can be expensive. A 

new approach, Hash-based Homomorphic Artificial Intelligence (HbHAI), uses key-dependent hash functions that 

preserve similarity properties, allowing AI algorithms to process data in its cryptographically secure form without 

modification and with improved performance compared to existing HE schemes. HbHAI invalidates the concept of 

decryption in HE, as only the data owner knows the correspondence between encrypted and unencrypted versions, 

allowing AI results to be transposed using only indexes. 

c. Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZKPs) 

Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZKPs) are cryptographic techniques that allow one party (the prover) to prove the truth of a 

statement to another party (the verifier) without revealing any information beyond the statement itself. In the context 

of Zero-Knowledge Machine Learning (ZKML), ZKPs enable effective validation of model performance and 

authenticity during both training and inference processes without disclosing sensitive data, thereby ensuring the 

verifiability and security of machine learning models. 

ZKML addresses growing concerns over data privacy and model security, especially when models are trained and 

deployed on cloud platforms or third-party servers. ZKP-based verifiable training ensures transparency and 

trustworthiness without revealing sensitive training data or model parameters. ZKP-based verifiable testing ensures 

model performance is within a certain range while protecting model parameter privacy. ZKP-based verifiable

4. Formal Verification of AI-Generated Cryptographic Components 

Formal verification techniques are crucial for ensuring the security and integrity of cryptographic protocols, which are 

often deployed without prior formal validation, leading to potential vulnerabilities. The increasing complexity and 

acceleration of new protocol development necessitate automated solutions for verification. Researchers are exploring 

the integration of symbolic reasoning tools with Large Language Model (LLM)-based agents to automate vulnerability 

detection in cryptographic protocols. 

This approach combines the rigorous deductive reasoning of formal verification systems (like the Tamarin Prover) 

with the adaptive capabilities of LLMs. LLMs can autonomously identify vulnerabilities in new cryptographic 

protocols, formalize them into the prover's syntax, and even intervene in proof searches by introducing support 

lemmas when the prover stalls. If an attack trace is found, the AI agent translates and validates it against the original 

protocol in a symbolic sandbox. This integration aims to overcome the complexity and time-consuming nature of 

traditional formal verification, offering a more automated and reliable approach to vulnerability detection. 

While this research focuses on LLMs identifying vulnerabilities in manually curated protocols, the broader implication 

is the potential for formal verification of AI-generated cryptographic components. The development of new 

cryptographic primitives like the Verifiable Evaluation Scheme (VE) for verifiable computation in AI and image 

processing further highlights this trend. These innovations improve the efficiency and scalability of cryptographic 

proof systems, ensuring the integrity, fairness, and privacy of data processing tasks. However, challenges remain in 

ensuring the trustworthiness of AI-designed cryptography, particularly regarding potential biases and the need for 

explainable AI (XAI) models to provide transparency into AI-driven cryptographic decisions.

D. Challenges and Ethical Considerations in AI-Based Cryptography 

The integration of AI into cryptography, while offering significant advancements, introduces a new set of challenges 

and ethical considerations that must be meticulously addressed. 

1. Computational Overhead and Scalability 

AI models, particularly deep learning architectures, are inherently computationally complex and resource intensive. 

This computational overhead can significantly impact the encryption speed and overall system efficiency, demanding 

substantial processing power. For instance, the training phase of AI models for cryptanalysis or design can require 

considerable time and computational resources. This poses scalability challenges, especially for organizations with 

limited resources, hindering the widespread adoption of AI-based cryptographic systems. While AI can optimize PQC 

implementations for faster execution and lower memory consumption, the initial training and ongoing operational

2. Data Dependency and Generalization 

Deep learning models require large-scale, high-quality training datasets for efficacy in cryptanalysis or cryptographic

resource intensive. For example, training robust side-channel attack models might necessitate millions of power traces,

information and the need for unrestricted access to devices. Existing datasets frequently lack diversity, leading to 

model overfitting, which severely impairs their effectiveness when encountering variations in device-specific noise 

patterns or cryptographic implementations in real-world scenarios. This inherent data-hungry nature and the 

challenges in generalization limit AI's practical effectiveness against diverse and protected cryptographic deployments. 

3. Interpretability and "Black Box" Problem 

Many AI models, particularly deep neural networks, function as "black boxes," meaning their internal decision-making 

processes are opaque and difficult to interpret. This lack of interpretability poses a significant challenge in 

cryptanalysis. When an AI model identifies correct key guesses or vulnerabilities, it is often unclear which specific 

cryptographic weaknesses the model is exploiting. This hinders cryptographers' ability to identify underlying 

vulnerabilities, improve algorithms, and understand the fundamental reasons for a cipher's compromise. It also 

increases the risk of false positives, as AI models might inadvertently rely on coincidental statistical correlations 

unrelated to actual cryptographic weaknesses. For AI-driven cryptographic designs, the "black box" nature makes it 

difficult to formally verify their security properties or ensure trustworthiness, as the rationale behind AI-generated 

parameters or structures is not transparent. The development of explainable AI (XAI) models is crucial to provide 

insights into how AI-generated cryptographic decisions are made, but substantial advancements are still needed. 

4. Adversarial AI and New Attack Vectors 

The very tools designed to enhance security can themselves become targets or sources of new vulnerabilities. AI 

systems can become security vulnerabilities if subjected to adversarial attacks or data poisoning during training 

phases, potentially producing intentionally misleading outcomes or compromising the integrity of AI-driven 

cryptographic designs. This creates a complex ethical and security dilemma. For instance, AI-generated encryption 

keys, while potentially more secure than traditional keys, may still be vulnerable to AI-driven attacks if not properly 

implemented. The increasing adoption of AI also expands potential attack surfaces, as adversaries might attempt to 

reverse-engineer AI-based cryptographic components to uncover sensitive embedded data or hidden vulnerabilities. 

Furthermore, the fundamental discrepancy between the discrete nature of cryptographic operations and the continuous 

computational model of Deep Neural Networks introduces a novel attack vector, where non-standard (non-binary) 

inputs can break DNN implementations of block ciphers, even full-round AES-128. This highlights that even strong 

ciphers can become vulnerable through improper AI integration. 

5. Ethical and Regulatory Concerns 

The integration of AI into cryptographic security raises significant ethical concerns, particularly regarding data privacy 

and the potential for algorithmic bias. Ensuring the ethical use of AI in cryptographic applications requires careful 

consideration and transparent, accountable AI-driven security measures. There are concerns about the potential misuse 

of AI-driven encryption methods by cybercriminals, highlighting AI's dual-use nature. This emphasizes the need for 

strict regulations to prevent malicious applications. Regulatory compliance remains a critical challenge, requiring 

governments and industry organizations to establish clear guidelines for the ethical use of AI in encryption and to 

ensure that security measures comply with data protection laws.

A. A Hybrid AI-Cryptographic Framework for Adaptive Security 

The proposed framework envisions a dynamic, self-optimizing cryptographic system that leverages AI to adapt its 

security posture in real-time. This moves beyond static, pre-configured security measures to a more resilient and 

responsive defence. 

AI-Driven Threat Intelligence and Anomaly Detection: At the core, AI models (e.g., deep learning networks, 

supervised and unsupervised learning algorithms) continuously monitor network traffic, system logs, and 

cryptographic operations for anomalies and emerging threat patterns. This includes detecting subtle deviations that 

might indicate side-channel attacks or attempts to exploit cryptographic weaknesses. The system would prioritize 

processing vast datasets at speeds unachievable by humans, enabling proactive threat identification and a shift from 

reactive to predictive security. 

Adaptive Cryptographic Parameter Adjustment: Based on the real-time threat intelligence, AI algorithms (e.g., 

reinforcement learning) would autonomously adjust cryptographic parameters. This could involve dynamically scaling 

encryption levels, modify key lengths, or switch between cryptographic algorithms to optimize security without 

sacrificing usability or performance. For instance, in low-threat environments, energy-efficient encryption could be 

prioritized, while high-threat scenarios would trigger robust, higher-complexity algorithms. This dynamic adaptation 

directly addresses the limitations of static cryptographic solutions against evolving threats. 

Automated Key Management and Optimization: AI would streamline and secure key management processes, 

historically a significant vulnerability point. This includes AI-driven generation of high-entropy keys, intelligent key 

distribution, and automated key rotation based on security policies and detected threats. Reinforcement learning could 

optimize key exchange protocols, minimize computational overhead and reduce latency, particularly for post-quantum 

key exchange. 

Integrated Side-Channel Attack Mitigation: The framework would incorporate AI-driven countermeasures for side-

channel attacks. This involves AI models analysing execution patterns to detect SCAs and dynamically optimizing 

mitigation techniques such as noise injection, adaptive randomization of operations, or fine-tuning masking schemes 

to balance security and computational overhead. 

B. Leveraging AI for Post-Quantum Cryptography Transition 

The looming threat of quantum computers necessitates a proactive transition to Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC). 

The proposed framework utilizes AI to facilitate this transition and enhance the security of PQC schemes. 

PQC Algorithm Optimization: AI, particularly reinforcement learning, can systematically optimize the 

implementation of PQC algorithms (e.g., lattice-based schemes like Kyber) for improved performance on modern 

hardware platforms. This involves fine-tuning critical implementation parameters, such as instruction scheduling, 

memory access patterns, and parallelization strategies, to reduce computational overhead and resource demands. This 

is crucial as PQC algorithms often introduce significant computational overhead compared to traditional cryptography. 

Quantum-Resistant Key Generation: The framework advocates for the integration of Quantum Random Number 

Generators (QRNGs) for key generation. AI can then be used to validate the randomness and entropy of these keys, 

ensuring they are free from statistical biases that AI-powered cryptanalysis might exploit. This provides maximum 

entropy across the entire key space, strengthening resistance against differential and sectional attacks. 

Hybrid Cryptographic Models: To ensure a seamless and secure transition, the framework proposes adopting hybrid 

encryption models that combine classical and post-quantum algorithms. AI can manage the selection and orchestration 

of these hybrid schemes, ensuring optimal security and performance during the transition phase. This approach allows 

organizations to prepare for quantum threats without immediately abandoning established classical systems. 

AI-Enhanced PQC Cryptanalysis: Recognizing AI's dual nature, the framework also includes AI-powered 

cryptanalysis tools specifically designed to evaluate the robustness of new PQC schemes. This involves using AI to 

identify potential vulnerabilities, optimize attack vectors, and empirically audit the security of PQC implementations, 

thereby strengthening their design before widespread deployment.

The "black box" nature of AI and the ethical concerns surrounding its deployment in critical security functions

Explainable AI (XAI) Integration: The framework prioritizes the integration of Explainable AI (XAI) models. These 

models would provide insights into how AI-driven cryptographic decisions are made, clarifying which patterns or 

weaknesses an AI model is exploiting in cryptanalysis, or why certain parameters are chosen in cryptographic design.

4. CONCLUSION 

The integration of Artificial Intelligence into cryptography marks a pivotal evolution in digital security, fundamentally 

reshaping both offensive and defensive paradigms. AI's unparalleled capabilities in rapid data processing, pattern 

recognition, and adaptive learning offer transformative potential for enhancing cryptographic systems against an 

increasingly sophisticated threat landscape. 

In cryptanalysis, AI has demonstrated remarkable success in distinguishing attacks and side-channel analysis, 

empirically identifying vulnerabilities in encryption schemes and significantly reducing the data and time required for 

attacks. The ability of AI to detect subtle statistical biases and exploit physical leakages highlights a critical gap 

between theoretical provable security and practical implementation security. While AI shows promise in key recovery 

for simplified ciphers, its effectiveness against full-round, modern cryptographic algorithms remains practically 

infeasible due to their high entropy, lack of exploitable patterns, and reliance on computationally hard problems. 

Furthermore, the inherent discrepancy between the discrete nature of cryptographic operations and the continuous 

computational model of deep neural networks introduces a novel vulnerability, allowing non-standard inputs to 

compromise DNN implementations of ciphers. 

Conversely, AI is proving to be an invaluable asset in cryptographic design and enhancement. It automates the design 

of complex primitives like S-boxes and hash functions, optimizes cryptographic algorithms, and enables adaptive 

encryption strategies that respond dynamically to real-time threats. AI-enhanced key exchange protocols promise 

greater efficiency and quantum resistance, while AI-driven mitigation strategies offer robust defences against side-

channel attacks. Crucially, AI is foundational to privacy-preserving cryptographic techniques such as Secure Multi-

Party Computation, Homomorphic Encryption, and Zero-Knowledge Proofs, enabling secure data processing and 

verifiable machine learning without compromising confidentiality. 

Despite these advancements, significant challenges persist. The computational overhead and data dependency of AI 

models, coupled with issues of generalization and interpretability, pose practical barriers to their widespread 

deployment in critical cryptographic functions. The "black box" nature of many AI models hinders the understanding 

of cryptographic weaknesses and introduces new security risks from adversarial AI. Ethical considerations 

surrounding data privacy, algorithmic bias, and the potential misuse of AI-powered cryptographic systems necessitate 

careful regulatory frameworks and a commitment to transparency. 

The proposed hybrid AI-cryptographic framework offers a conceptual roadmap for future development, emphasizing 

adaptive security, a seamless transition to post-quantum cryptography, and enhanced trustworthiness through 

Explainable AI and formal verification. This integrated approach acknowledges AI's dual role, leveraging its strengths 

to build more resilient and privacy-centric cryptographic solutions while proactively addressing its inherent limitations 

and ethical implications. The ongoing synergy between AI and cryptography is not merely an incremental 

improvement but a fundamental shift towards a more intelligent, adaptive, and robust digital security ecosystem for 

the future.
